# Vision

## The problem

Every claims question becomes a phone call, and every phone call becomes
manual investigation. The rep isn't blind — they can find the answer — but
finding it means interpreting denial codes, chasing down a missing ROI
authorization, and cross-referencing coverage and prior-auth rules by hand,
for every single caller. That manual work is what drives handle time up and
first-call resolution down.

And it's all reactive. A claim headed for denial — missing referral, wrong
modifier, a CPT mismatch, eligibility timing — isn't caught until it's
already failed and locked into a 30–60 day reprocessing cycle. By then the
member has already called once to ask what happened, and often calls again
when the first answer wasn't quite complete.

## The shift

Claim Assist automates the investigation, not just the conversation. The AI
does the lookup work a rep would otherwise do by hand — reading the claim,
checking ROI, resolving coverage and prior-auth questions — and answers the
member directly when it can. A rep only picks up when the situation actually
needs a person, and inherits a call that's already been triaged instead of
one they have to start from zero. Risk gets flagged before submission, not
explained after denial.

## Who it's for

- **The member** — calls or texts in and talks to the AI first. No hold
  queue, no repeating themselves, plain-language answers about their claim.
- **The rep** — picks up from the queue when the AI hands off. Inherits
  context, not a cold call: what's been asked, what's been found, why it
  escalated.
- **The manager** — watches the shift happen in aggregate. How much the AI
  resolved on its own vs. handed off, where claims are flagged before
  denial, where the process is still slow.

## What good looks like

A member gets a straight answer without waiting for a human. A rep who does
get pulled in already knows what the member needs. A manager can point at
the dashboard and see, in real numbers, how much of the call center's work
the AI carried on its own.
